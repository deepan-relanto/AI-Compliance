The one-sentence version

If AI helps produce a decision, document, or message — you own the outcome.

Use approved tools, check for fairness and accuracy, be honest about AI's role, and keep a human meaningfully in charge.

Not a legal-only policy

Responsible AI is everyday judgement: what you ask, what you check, what you disclose, when you escalate.

Scale, trust, accountability

A biased process can affect thousands of cases. “The AI did it” is never a defense.

Definition05 / 20
Common misconceptions

These myths quietly create ethics and compliance risk.

01

“AI is neutral — it's just math”

AI learns from human data, including unfair patterns. Math reproduces what it is given.

02

“Ethics is Legal's job”

Policies set boundaries; most decisions are what you ask, check, and send.

03

“If the tool allows it, it's fine”

Tools rarely enforce ethics. Capability is not permission.

04

“Responsible means avoid AI”

It means deliberate use: right task, oversight, honest disclosure.

05

“Bias only matters in hiring”

Bias shows up in imagery, prioritization, summaries, tone, and more.

06

“If I didn't write it, I'm not responsible”

You own everything you submit, send, or publish — whatever drafted it.

Core principles08 / 20
Fairness and Bias

Bias is systematic unfairness — usually learned from history, not coded on purpose.

A major tech company scrapped a CV-screening tool that penalized résumés associated with women — it had learned from a decade of male-dominated hiring data.

  • Image tools that always depict “CEO” or “nurse” with one gender or ethnicity
  • Job adverts whose tone skews masculine or feminine
  • Summaries that credit some speakers and bury others
  • Customer prioritization that tracks postcode or name patterns
Practical habit: if output involves people, ask — would this look different for another gender, ethnicity, age, or background? Unsure? Get a second pair of eyes.
Fairness and bias09 / 20
Three pillars

Be open, be able to explain, and never blame the software.

Transparency

Disclose to recipients (bots), within teams, and in published content when trust depends on origin.

Explainability

Hiring, promotion, claims, discipline: give job-relevant reasons without citing “the algorithm.”

Accountability

Software cannot apologize to a regulator. Responsibility stays with people and the organization.

Memorize: you can delegate the typing to AI. You cannot delegate the responsibility.
Human accountability10 / 20
Human Accountability

Oversight must be real — not a rubber stamp.

Automation bias

People over-trust machine suggestions and approve faster than a colleague's work.

Meaningful control

Humans need time, information, and authority to disagree with the AI.

Low

Internal brainstorm / rough draft → quick read-through

Medium

Client email / published copy → full fact-check + approval chain

High

People decisions / legal or financial / public → documented human decision

Oversight by stakes11 / 20
Ethical Decision Framework (PACT)

Before sensitive AI use, run the PACT check.

P

Permission

Allowed by policy? Approved tool? Is the data appropriate to share with it?

A

Accuracy & fairness

Verified the output? Could it be biased against anyone affected?

C

Candor

Would you be comfortable if everyone affected knew exactly how AI was used?

T

Takeover

Is a human still making the consequential decision, with authority to overrule?

Any “no” or “not sure”? Pause — adjust, or ask your manager, HR, Legal, or AI policy owner.

Section13 / 20
Misuse

Deception is the bright line

01

Deception

Fake testimonials, fabricated case studies, impersonating a real person without authorization.

02

Manipulation

Misleading content, astroturfing, exploiting pressure at scale.

03

Harassment

Mocking, demeaning, or fabricating claims about colleagues or others.

04

Dishonesty

Passing off AI analysis as independent expert work where authenticity is expected.

05

Circumvention

Tricking tools past safety rules, or using unapproved tools to dodge controls.

Misuse types14 / 20
Deepfakes

Never create unauthorized likenesses — and never trust urgency on voice alone.

Never create

Synthesizing a colleague's, executive's, or customer's voice or face without explicit authorization is off-limits — even “as a joke.”

Stay alert receiving

Cloned voices and deepfaked video calls have driven fraudulent transfers. Confirm urgent requests out-of-band.

  • Voice cloning — “I recognized the voice” is obsolete as verification
  • Likeness impersonation — faces and video can be faked convincingly
  • Urgency + secrecy — “do this now, tell no one” is a classic red flag
  • Out-of-band verification — call a saved number; confirm in person; report
Deepfakes15 / 20
Copyright

Three questions cover most everyday situations

Input

Don't feed tools content you lack rights to use (full paid reports, competitors' copyrighted material).

Output

AI can echo existing works or styles. Commercial use needs originality and brand checks.

Ownership

Purely AI-generated works may have weak copyright. Brand-critical assets: involve Legal.

“The AI wrote it” does not make copying song lyrics, books, or paid content lawful.

Workplace Best Practices

Habits that keep AI use ethical at Relanto

01

Label chatbots — identify virtual assistants; never claim to be human.

02

Disclose AI assistance where policy or trust requires it.

03

Source benchmarks and stats — no AI-invented “industry facts.”

04

Informed consent for likeness — voice/face only with approval.

05

Report systemic bias — document patterns; tell the tool owner.

06

Match review to impact — more scrutiny when people, money, or reputation are at stake.

Best practices17 / 20
Dos and Don'ts

Quick reference for daily decisions

Do

  • Keep humans in charge of people, money, and public commitments
  • Check outputs about people for skew
  • Identify bots; disclose when trust depends on it
  • Verify claims and citations before sending
  • Verify urgent requests out-of-band; report suspected fraud
  • Run PACT for sensitive tasks

Don't

  • Let a score replace explainable judgement
  • Assume “the machine is objective”
  • Let a bot claim to be human
  • Ship hallucinated benchmarks into decision docs
  • Create synthetic media of real people without consent
  • Act on urgency-plus-secrecy alone
